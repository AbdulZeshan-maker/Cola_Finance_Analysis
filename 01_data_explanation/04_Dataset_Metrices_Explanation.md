### **All Metrices Explanation**





##### **Profit and Loss Statement:**



**Let’s walk through this profit and loss statement again, this time putting it directly into the context of The Coca-Cola Company. When you are pulling datasets like this to build financial reports or run forecasting models, understanding exactly what each line item represents in the real world is essential for accurate analysis.**



**Here is how the P\&L waterfall looks for a massive beverage brand:**



**1. The Top Line (The Money Coming In)**

**NET OPERATING REVENUES: This is the total money Coca-Cola brings in globally from selling its core products. For Coke, this mostly comes from selling beverage concentrates and syrups to their bottling partners, as well as selling finished drinks (like Coke, Sprite, and Dasani) directly to retailers.**



**Example: Retailers and bottling plants pay Coca-Cola for shipments of syrup and bottled drinks.**



**2. The Direct Costs**

**Cost of goods sold (COGS): The direct costs to manufacture those syrups and beverages.**



**Example: The cost of raw ingredients (water, sugar, caramel color, the secret formula), the aluminum for cans, the plastic for bottles, and the wages of the factory workers making the products.**



**Gross Profit: Revenue minus COGS. This shows the profit Coke makes strictly from producing and selling its beverages, before any corporate overhead is factored in.**



**3. The Operating Expenses**

**Selling, general and administrative expenses (SG\&A): Coca-Cola is famous for its massive overhead, particularly in marketing. This line captures the everyday costs of running the global empire.**



**Example: Billions spent on global marketing and advertising (like Super Bowl commercials and Olympic sponsorships), salaries for corporate executives in Atlanta, and the costs of running their global sales teams.**



**Other operating charges: Unusual or non-routine costs related to their operations.**



**Example: Costs associated with restructuring a regional division, severance pay for layoffs, or write-downs on factory equipment that is no longer being used.**



**Operating Income: Gross Profit minus SG\&A and other charges. This is the true profit generated by Coca-Cola's core business of selling drinks, excluding taxes and financial maneuvers.**



**4. The Side Hustles and Financials**

**Interest income: The money Coca-Cola earns on the massive amounts of cash it holds in its bank accounts globally.**



**Interest expense: Coca-Cola often issues corporate bonds (borrowing money from investors) to fund expansions or buy other companies. This is the interest they pay out on that debt.**



**Equity income (loss) - net: Coca-Cola doesn't own all its bottlers; it often just buys a percentage stake in them (like Coca-Cola Euro pacific Partners) or takes a stake in other brands (like Monster Beverage). Coke’s percentage share of those other companies' profits or losses is recorded here.**



**Other income (loss) - net: Non-operating financial gains or losses.**



**Example: Because Coke operates globally, a lot of this comes from currency exchange fluctuations. It could also include money made from selling off a piece of corporate real estate.**



**5. Taxes and The Bottom Line**

**Income before income taxes: The Operating Income plus or minus all the interest, equity, and other financial items mentioned above.**



**Income taxes: The corporate taxes Coca-Cola owes to various governments around the world based on the income they generated.**



**CONSOLIDATED NET INCOME: The total, after-tax profit for the entire Coca-Cola system being reported on this document.**



**6. The Final Adjustments**

**Less: Net income attributable to noncontrolling interests: Sometimes Coca-Cola owns a majority stake (say, 85%) in a specific regional bottling subsidiary, meaning they control it and lump 100% of its profits into the "Consolidated Net Income" line above. This line deducts the 15% of those profits that actually belong to the minority partners.**



**Net income from continuing operations: The profit generated only by the ongoing, healthy parts of Coca-Cola’s business.**



**Income (Loss) from Discontinued Operations...: If Coca-Cola decides to completely abandon a specific business line, the final financial impact of shutting it down goes here.**



**Example: If Coke had a separate snack food division that they decided to completely shut down and sell off this year, the final losses or gains from that closed division would sit here so it doesn't distort the performance of their ongoing beverage business.**



**NET INCOME ATTRIBUTABLE TO SHAREOWNERS OF THE COCA-COLA COMPANY: The absolute bottom line. After paying for the ingredients, the marketing, the debt, the taxes, and the minority partners, this is the final profit that belongs solely to the actual shareholders of Coca-Cola stock.**





##### **Balance Sheet Statement:**



**While a Profit and Loss statement shows you a video of how much money a company made over a whole year, a Balance Sheet is a snapshot taken on a single day (usually December 31st). It shows exactly what Coca-Cola owns (Assets), what it owes (Liabilities), and what is left over for the owners (Equity).**



**The golden rule of a balance sheet is that Assets = Liabilities + Equity.**



**1. Current Assets (What Coke owns that turns into cash within a year)**

**Cash and cash equivalents: The actual cash sitting in Coca-Cola's bank accounts globally, plus ultra-safe, short-term items that are basically as good as cash.**



**Short-term investments: Money Coke parked in investments (like treasury bills) that they plan to cash out within 12 months.**



**Marketable securities: Stocks or bonds of other companies that Coke bought and can quickly sell on the open market if they need cash.**



**Trade accounts receivable, less allowances: The money bottling companies and major retailers (like Walmart) owe Coca-Cola for shipments of syrup and drinks they have already received. The "less allowances" part is Coke admitting that a few of those customers will probably default and never pay.**



**Inventories: The physical stuff sitting in warehouses. For Coke, this is raw sugar, aluminum cans, secret formula ingredients, and pallets of finished soda ready to ship.**



**Prepaid expenses and other assets: Bills Coke paid in advance. For example, prepaying a year's worth of insurance for their global delivery fleet, or paying for Super Bowl ads months before the game airs.**



**Assets held for sale: A part of the business or physical property (like an old syrup factory) that Coke is actively trying to sell off right now.**



**Total current assets: The sum of all the short-term items above.**



**2. Long-Term Assets (What Coke owns for the long haul)**

**Equity method investments: Coca-Cola doesn't own all of its bottlers. Often, they buy a large chunk (like 25%) of a bottling partner. The value of those partial ownership stakes goes here.**



**Property, plant and equipment - net: The physical empire. This is the value of all their manufacturing plants, corporate office buildings, and delivery trucks, minus the wear-and-tear (depreciation) they suffer over time.**



**Deferred income tax assets: Situations where Coke overpaid its taxes in the past and gets to use those credits to lower future tax bills.**



**TRADEMARKS WITH INDEFINITE LIVES: The literal brand names—Coca-Cola, Sprite, Fanta, Dasani. These are incredibly valuable assets, and because the Coke brand doesn't just expire after 10 years, it has an "indefinite life."**



**BOTTLERS' FRANCHISE RIGHTS WITH INDEFINITE LIVES: The value of the exclusive, lifelong legal contracts Coke has given to certain bottlers to operate in specific global regions.**



**Goodwill: When Coca-Cola buys another brand (like when they bought Costa Coffee or vitaminwater), they usually pay more than the brand's raw physical assets are worth. That extra premium paid for the brand's reputation and customer loyalty is recorded as "Goodwill."**



**Other intangible assets: Valuable things Coke owns that you cannot physically touch, like patents on a new bottle shape or proprietary corporate software.**



**Total assets: The grand total of everything Coca-Cola owns (Current + Long-Term).**



**3. Current Liabilities (What Coke owes within the next 12 months)**

**Accounts payable and accrued expenses: The unpaid bills. This is the money Coke owes to its suppliers (e.g., the company that sells them cardboard boxes) and the wages they currently owe their employees.**



**Loans and notes payable: Short-term borrowing, like drawing on a line of credit from a bank to smooth out day-to-day cash flow.**



**Current maturities of long-term debt: Let's say Coke took out a huge 10-year loan a while ago. The specific portion of that massive loan that must be paid back this year is recorded here.**



**Accrued income taxes: The corporate taxes Coke owes to governments worldwide for the current year, but hasn't actually written the check for yet.**



**Total current liabilities: The total sum of all Coke's short-term debts.**



**4. Long-Term Liabilities (Debts due after 12 months)**

**Long-term debt: Massive corporate bonds or loans Coke uses to fund huge projects (like building new factories or buying other companies) that won't be paid off for many years.**



**Other liabilities: Long-term obligations like employee pension funds.**



**Deferred income taxes: Taxes that Coke legally owes based on their current income, but due to accounting rules, they won't actually have to pay to the government until future years.**



**5. Shareholders' Equity (The owners' slice of the pie)**

**Common stock: The base, initial value of the shares Coca-Cola issued to its investors when creating the stock.**



**Capital surplus: The extra money investors paid for those shares above their base (par) value.**



**Reinvested earnings: This is a massive number for old companies like Coke. It represents all the profits the company has made since it was founded that it kept in the business to grow, rather than paying it out to shareholders as cash dividends.**



**Accumulated other comprehensive income (loss): Because Coke operates globally in dozens of currencies, this line mostly tracks "paper" gains or losses from fluctuating foreign exchange rates.**



**Treasury stock, at cost: Coca-Cola frequently buys its own stock back from the open market. This number is negative because it reduces the total equity pie.**



**EQUITY ATTRIBUTABLE TO SHAREOWNERS OF THE COCA-COLA COMPANY: If you sold every single Coca-Cola asset and used the cash to pay off every single Coca-Cola debt, this is the net worth that belongs to the actual shareholders.**



**Equity attributable to noncontrolling interests: The slice of equity belonging to minority partners in smaller subsidiaries that Coca-Cola controls.**



**Total liabilities and equity: The grand total of everything owed plus everything belonging to shareholders. This bottom number will always exactly match the "Total assets" number above.**





##### **Cash Flow Statement:**



**When you are pulling this kind of data into reporting tools or building financial forecasting models, the Cash Flow Statement is arguably the most critical table. While the Profit \& Loss statement includes "paper" accounting (like depreciation or promises to pay later), the Cash Flow statement strictly tracks the physical dollars entering and leaving Coca-Cola's bank accounts.**



**It is divided into three main sections: Operating, Investing, and Financing.**



**1. Cash from Operating Activities (The Core Business)**

**This section starts with your bottom-line profit from the P\&L and adjusts it to figure out how much actual cash was generated by selling beverages.**



**CONSOLIDATED NET INCOME: The starting point. This is the final profit pulled directly from the bottom of the P\&L statement.**



**Depreciation and amortization: On the P\&L, Coke subtracts the wear-and-tear of its delivery trucks and factories as an expense. However, no actual cash left the bank account this year for that wear-and-tear. This line adds that money back to the cash total.**



**Stock-based compensation expense: Coke often pays executives in stock shares rather than cash bonuses. This adds that value back because cash wasn't actually spent.**



**Deferred income taxes: The tax expense Coke reported on the P\&L, but legally gets to push off paying until a future year. Since the cash is still in their bank account, it gets added back.**



**Equity (income) loss - net of dividends: Adjustments for paper profits or losses Coke claimed from bottling partners they partially own, minus any actual cash dividends those partners paid out to them.**



**Foreign currency adjustments: Because Coke operates globally, the value of cash sitting in foreign banks changes when converted back to US Dollars.**



**Significant (gains) losses on sales of assets / Other significant (gains) losses / Other operating charges: Adjustments for unusual, one-off events so they don't skew the view of the core day-to-day cash generation.**



**Net change in operating assets and liabilities: This is the day-to-day working capital. If Coke spent a lot of cash hoarding raw sugar (inventory), this number goes down. If a massive retailer finally paid their overdue invoice, this number goes up.**



**Net cash provided by operating activities: The total actual cash generated simply by running the Coca-Cola beverage empire this year.**



**2. Cash from Investing Activities (Buying and Selling Assets)**

**This section tracks the cash Coca-Cola spends to grow its empire for the future, or the cash it gets from selling off pieces of it.**



**Purchases / Proceeds of short-term investments: Cash Coke used to buy safe, short-term assets (like government bonds) to earn a little yield, or the cash they got when those bonds matured.**



**Purchases / Proceeds from disposals of investments: Cash spent buying, or cash received selling, longer-term financial investments.**



**Acquisitions / Proceeds from disposals of businesses, equity method investments...: The massive amounts of cash Coke spends to buy out other brands (like acquiring a sparkling water company), or the cash they make if they sell off a bottling plant they used to own.**



**Purchases of property, plant and equipment: Often called CapEx (Capital Expenditures). This is the heavy cash spent building new syrup manufacturing plants, buying new fleets of delivery trucks, or upgrading office buildings.**



**Proceeds from disposals of property, plant and equipment: Cash made from selling off old, used assets, like auctioning off outdated factory equipment.**



**Net cash provided by (used in) investing activities: The total cash drained from or added to the bank account from all these investments. This number is almost always negative for a growing company because they are constantly spending cash to build new things.**



**3. Cash from Financing Activities (Funding the Empire)**

**This section tracks how Coca-Cola raises cash from banks or Wall Street, and how it returns cash to its owners (the shareholders).**



**Issuances of debt: The massive influx of cash Coke receives when it takes out a new corporate loan or issues bonds to investors.**



**Payments of debt: The cash drained from the account to pay off old loans or bonds that have finally matured.**



**Issuances of stock: Cash injected into the company when Coke creates and sells new shares of stock to the public.**



**Purchases of stock for treasury: Often called "Stock Buybacks." This is the cash Coke spends on the open market buying its own stock back to increase the value of the remaining shares.**



**Dividends: The literal cash checks Coca-Cola writes and mails to its shareholders every quarter as a reward for owning the stock.**



**Net cash provided by (used in) financing activities: The total net cash flow from borrowing, paying down debt, and rewarding shareholders.**



**4. The Final Reconciliations**

**Cash Provided by (Used in) Operating/Investing/Financing Activities, Discontinued Operations: If Coke completely shut down a division (like a failed snack food experiment), the cash flows specifically tied to that dead division are neatly separated here so they don't corrupt the data for the ongoing business.**



**Effect of exchange rate changes on cash and cash equivalents: The final true-up of how currency fluctuations across the globe impacted the US Dollar value of their total cash pile.**



**Net increase (decrease) during the year: The ultimate bottom line of the statement. If you sum up all the operating cash, minus the investing cash, plus or minus the financing cash, this is exactly how much thicker or thinner Coca-Cola's wallet got between January 1st and December 31st.**
